Mesoscopic Magnetic Imaging
Electrons create magnetic fields, so materials that manifest quantum mechanical and strongly correlated electron behavior must have magnetic signatures. Although distinctive, interesting, and informative, these signatures are hard to measure. Our lab group develops specialized magnetic nano probes such as scanning SQUIDs, force microscopes, and Hall probes to image the magnetic fields produced by electrons. These probes are a great tool for basic condensed matter studies because emergent phenomena in quantum systems exhibit magnetic signatures. We can observe subtle effects on fundamental mesoscopic length scales and distinguish unanticipated spatial variation from genuinely new functionalities. Our SQUIDs and force microscopes can also manipulate magnetic phenomena to observe their static and dynamic responses.
Quantum Materials
Electrons seem deceptively simple: elementary fermions with charge e and spin ½. Put them in a quantum material, and things get interesting. Their mass is small enough that their wavelike nature is noticeable at energies that are accessible in many laboratories. They may be strongly correlated, meaning that what each electron does strongly affects other electrons. They move through a material environment that may have lattice, spin, charge, and orbital degrees of freedom — properties that depend on the atomic composition and crystal structure of the material itself. By appropriate optimization of these parameters, we can create a variety of different phases. In the environment of a real material, many factors contribute to the formation of spatial variation: quantum mechanical behavior, strong interactions, complicated lattices, competing phases, and susceptibility to disorder. With modern imaging methods, we can see this spatial variation directly, quantify it, and even manipulate it. These methods allow us to find and study astonishing phenomena and functionalities — from diamagnetism and vortices in exotic superconductors, to dissipationless conductivity at the boundaries of topological insulators, to emergent particles with fractional charge and statistics.
Quantum Devices
Quantum computers may soon be able to perform complicated calculations that are not possible on classical computers. However, the quantum bits — qubits — that have been built so far are susceptible to noise from the environment, which can cause errors in their calculations. So-called topological qubits may be less prone to error. We are investigating a variety of paths to quantum devices that could function as topological qubits.

Research Education
These highly demanding experiments are well-suited to train some of the nation’s top young scientists in an intellectually rich and supportive research environment. Members of the Moler group learn nanotechnology and precision measurement techniques as well as fundamental physics. Our alumni go on to a broad range of high-impact positions, from becoming a full professor of physics in Germany at age 29 to founding (and leading as CEO) a funded startup company that makes field-deployable technology to give immediate answers on soil nutrient needs, aiming to impact farming sustainability and affordability.
